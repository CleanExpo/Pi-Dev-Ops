## Email 2 — Day 3: Your first job — do it right

**Subject:** One job done right changes everything.
**Preview text:** Here's exactly what to capture on your first RestoreAssist job.

---

Hi [First Name],

Three days in. Have you run a job through RestoreAssist yet?

If yes — great. If not — let me make it simple.

Your first job through the system is the most important one. Not because anything will go wrong. Because it sets the standard. Your techs will watch how you do it. Your clients will receive the report. You'll get a feel for the workflow. And if something does go sideways with a claim three months from now, you'll have a record that holds.

**On your next job, capture these six things:**

1. **Initial moisture readings** — before you touch anything. Date and time auto-stamps.
2. **Before photos** — every affected area, every room. Don't curate. Capture everything.
3. **Scope notes** — what you found, what's been agreed, what the plan is. In plain language.
4. **Drying logs** — daily readings. RestoreAssist formats these to IICRC S500 standard automatically.
5. **Progress photos** — mid-drying documentation matters more than most people realise.
6. **Completion record** — final readings, final photos, client sign-off.

That's it. Six things. They take less time than you think, and they're the difference between getting paid and getting into a dispute.

**One thing I want you to avoid:**

Don't treat RestoreAssist like a reporting tool you fill in after the job. Use it on-site, in real time. The timestamps matter. The sequence matters. If you document after the fact, you lose the evidentiary weight that makes the record worth anything.

Your documentation should look like it was captured as the job happened. Because it was.

Go do a job the right way.

— Klark

## Email 3 — Day 7: How to use RestoreAssist to document a scope dispute

**Subject:** The adjuster says it wasn't in scope. Here's what you do next.
**Preview text:** Practical steps. Real scenario. What to pull from RestoreAssist when you need it.

---

Hi [First Name],

Let's talk about the scenario no one wants but almost everyone faces.

You completed a job. You sent the invoice. The claim officer — or an assessor who turned up three weeks after you dried the structure — says the scope was too broad. They want to reduce the payout.

Here's what most contractors do: argue. Send more emails. Wait.

Here's what you're going to do: pull the file.

**The RestoreAssist dispute file — what to send:**

**1. The initial moisture survey**
Date-stamped, geo-tagged, room-by-room. This documents what you found when you arrived. This is your baseline. If someone says the damage wasn't that extensive, your initial readings say otherwise.

**2. The drying log sequence**
Every reading, every day, in IICRC-compliant format. This shows the progression. It shows the equipment was necessary. It shows the drying was active and tracked.

**3. The photo timeline**
Before, during, and after — timestamped and sequential. An adjuster who wasn't on-site cannot contradict a photo taken at 7:14am on the day of initial assessment.

**4. The scope agreement record**
What was agreed to before work commenced. If you captured it in RestoreAssist at job setup, it's in the file. Date-stamped. No ambiguity.

**5. The completion sign-off**
Client signature on completion. If the property owner was satisfied when you left, that matters.

**How to export:**
Go to the job → Tap "Generate Dispute Report" → Select the date range → Export as PDF.

Send that PDF to the insurer. Not an email. A PDF. A document with a cover page, sequential records, and your company letterhead.

That's what a professional looks like. That's what makes the adjuster's position harder to hold.

Most scope disputes get resolved before they escalate when the restorer can produce a complete, chronological file. Most restorers can't produce that file. You can.

Go build your file.

— Klark
