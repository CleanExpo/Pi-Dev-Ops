## Part 1: What Is AEO and Why It Matters Now

**Title:** AEO: The Search Shift ANZ Businesses Can't Afford to Miss

---

Ten years ago, every business owner learned about SEO. Rank on Google, get found. Simple enough.

The shift happening right now is bigger — and faster.

Answer Engine Optimisation (AEO) is the practice of structuring your content and digital presence so that AI-powered search tools cite your business when answering user questions. Instead of returning a list of links, platforms like ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google's AI Overviews synthesise an answer directly — and either your business is named in that answer, or it isn't.

**Why this matters right now, not in three years:**

The adoption numbers are moving quickly. AI assistant usage has grown from novelty to default for a significant portion of the 25–45 age group — the core decision-making demographic for most B2B trade purchases. When a site manager is comparing concrete suppliers between jobs, or an operations manager is researching electrical wholesalers on their phone, there's a real and growing probability they're asking an AI rather than typing into a search bar.

Traditional SEO still matters. It isn't going away. But AEO is an additional channel — one where the competition is currently low and the opportunity to build early authority is significant.

For ANZ businesses specifically, the timing is advantageous. The Australian and New Zealand markets are roughly 18 months behind the US on AEO adoption by businesses. Your buyers are already behaving like AI search users. Many of your competitors haven't responded yet.

**What AEO is not:**

It's not a technical overhaul of your website. It's not a new advertising platform. It's not requiring you to learn a new discipline from scratch.

AEO is primarily about three things: content consistency, structured expertise signals, and cross-platform brand coherence. All three are achievable for a small business. All three are things you should be doing anyway for brand presence.

**Key Takeaway:** AEO is not the future of search — it's the present. ANZ businesses that start building for it now are doing so in a less competitive environment than will exist in 24 months.

## Part 2: How ChatGPT and Perplexity Decide What to Recommend

**Title:** Inside the Black Box: How AI Search Engines Choose Which Businesses to Cite

---

You've asked ChatGPT a question and noticed it gave you a specific recommendation — a brand, a service provider, a product. Have you wondered how it decided?

The process isn't arbitrary. AI search engines follow a pattern of evidence-based authority assessment that, once you understand it, becomes very actionable.

**The three primary citation signals:**

**1. Cross-platform consistency**

AI search engines don't just look at your website. They scan the broader web: your LinkedIn, your Google Business profile, review platforms, industry directories, social media, press mentions. They're asking: does this business appear coherently and consistently across the digital landscape, or is it fragmented and unclear?

A business with a strong, consistent presence across five platforms will be cited significantly more reliably than a business with an excellent website but minimal elsewhere.

**2. Explicit expertise signals**

What do you actually know? AI engines look for content that demonstrates domain expertise — not generic blog posts, but content that makes specific, verifiable claims in your category. A concrete supplier who has published detailed content on mix specifications, regional aggregate sourcing, and structural applications will outperform a competitor whose only content is "we sell quality concrete."

The expertise needs to be specific. Specific content creates clear entity signals: this business is an authority in *this category*, in *this region*.

**3. Social proof and third-party validation**

Reviews, testimonials, case studies, media mentions, industry association memberships — all of these contribute to what AI engines assess as trustworthiness. It's not just about volume (100 generic reviews) but about specificity and recency.

**What AI engines don't weigh heavily:**

Design quality. Ad spend. Follower counts on social media. These may matter for other reasons, but they don't directly influence AI citation decisions.

**The practical implication:**

A small, well-structured ANZ trade business can outperform a much larger but poorly structured competitor in AI search recommendations. Size isn't the primary variable. Structure and consistency are.

**Key Takeaway:** AI search citation is earned through cross-platform consistency, specific expertise signals, and credible social proof — all of which are achievable for an SMB with the right system.

## Part 3: Making Your Content AI-Citation-Ready

**Title:** Citation-Ready Content: The Structural Changes That Make AI Engines Recommend You

---

Most business content is not structured for AI citation. That's not a criticism — it's just that content has historically been written for human readers and Google's algorithm. AI search engines evaluate content differently.

Here's what citation-ready content actually looks like — and how to create it without a complete overhaul.

**Structure 1: The Expertise Claim + Evidence pattern**

AI engines favour content that makes a specific claim and backs it with specifics. Generic statements are filtered out. Specific, verifiable ones are indexed as expertise signals.

Weak: "We provide quality electrical supplies to Brisbane businesses."
Citation-ready: "We supply commercial-grade electrical components to Queensland contractors, with same-day dispatch from our Archerfield warehouse on orders placed before 2pm."

The second version tells an AI engine: this business is in electrical supply, they serve Queensland contractors, they operate in the Archerfield area, they offer same-day dispatch. Multiple entity signals in one sentence.

**Structure 2: FAQ and question-answer formatting**

AI engines are optimised to pull answers to specific questions. Content structured as direct question-and-answer pairs is significantly more likely to be cited. A page on your website with a genuine FAQ — not fluffy, not generic, but questions your actual customers ask — creates multiple citation opportunities.

"What's the lead time for custom orders?" with a specific, honest answer is more citable than two paragraphs of vague "we work hard to meet your needs" copy.

**Structure 3: Location + category specificity**

AI search for trade and service businesses is heavily location-weighted. "Best plumbing wholesaler in Melbourne" pulls different results than "best plumbing wholesaler." Your content needs to consistently signal what you do AND where you do it — across every platform, not just your website.

**Structure 4: Consistent entity mentions**

Your business name should appear consistently across platforms — spelled identically, paired with the same core category description. If Google Business says "ACE Electrical Supplies" and your LinkedIn says "Ace Electrical" and your website says "ACE Electrical Pty Ltd," you've created entity ambiguity that reduces AI citation confidence.

**Key Takeaway:** Citation-ready content is specific, structured, location-anchored, and consistent — not necessarily more content, but better-constructed content.

## Part 4: The Dual-Algorithm Strategy (Google + AI Search Simultaneously)

**Title:** One Strategy, Two Engines: How to Rank on Google and Get Cited by AI at the Same Time

---

A common misconception about AEO: you have to choose between optimising for Google and optimising for AI search. In practice, the two strategies are largely complementary — and the businesses winning at both aren't running two separate approaches.

Here's why they overlap — and where the distinctions matter.

**What Google and AI engines share:**

Both reward content that demonstrates genuine expertise. Both favour businesses with consistent signals across multiple platforms. Both weight local relevance for location-specific queries. Both penalise thin, generic, or incoherent content.

The core principle — publish quality, structured, consistent content that clearly defines your expertise in your category — serves both engines simultaneously.

**Where they diverge:**

Google's algorithm still heavily weights technical SEO: page load speed, backlink authority, keyword density in specific locations (title tags, H1s, meta descriptions), and indexed page count. These technical factors matter less to AI search engines, which are more focused on semantic content quality and entity authority.

AI search engines weight conversational structure more heavily. Content that directly answers a question — in plain language, with specifics — is more likely to be cited than keyword-optimised content that doesn't read naturally.

**The practical dual-algorithm content approach:**

1. Start with genuine expertise content (what do your customers actually need to know?)
2. Structure it with clear headings, specific claims, and FAQ formatting (serves both)
3. Add technical SEO basics: proper title, meta description, local keyword signals (serves Google primarily)
4. Publish consistently across platforms with consistent entity mentions (serves AI primarily)
5. Earn structured social proof: reviews, case studies, third-party mentions (serves both)

**The compounding advantage:**

When you do this well, Google performance feeds AI citation. Google ranking increases your domain authority, which AI engines use as a quality signal. AI citations increase branded search volume, which improves Google performance. The two engines reinforce each other when your content foundation is solid.

**For ANZ trade businesses specifically:**

The local SEO components are particularly valuable. Local pack ranking on Google and location-based AI citation both stem from the same foundation: consistent local entity signals, active Google Business profile, genuine local reviews. You're building one asset that performs across two search environments.

**Key Takeaway:** The dual-algorithm strategy isn't twice the work — it's building one solid content foundation with proper structure that serves both Google and AI search engines simultaneously.

## Part 5: Your 30-Day AEO Action Plan

**Title:** 30 Days to AI-Citation-Ready: A Practical Plan for ANZ Business Owners

---

This is the part where theory becomes action. Here's a realistic 30-day plan for an ANZ trade business owner who has no dedicated marketing team and limited time.

The goal at the end of 30 days: a structured digital presence that is actively building AI citation authority — and a system that maintains it without requiring ongoing heavy lifting.

---

**Days 1–5: Audit and Foundation**

Assess where you currently stand before adding anything new.

- List every platform where your business has a presence (Google Business, LinkedIn, Facebook, Instagram, industry directories, review platforms)
- Check for entity consistency: is your business name, category description, and location spelled identically across all of them? Fix any discrepancies — this single step often moves AEO scores measurably
- Check your Google Business profile: is it complete? Recent photos? Updated hours? This is your highest-leverage single asset for local AI search
- Identify your top 3 customer questions — the things people ask you before deciding to buy. Write them down. They'll become content.

**Days 6–10: Content Foundation**

Build the core content assets that AI engines pull from most reliably.

- Write (or update) your core services/products page with specific, location-anchored language. Use the Expertise Claim + Evidence structure from Part 3
- Create a genuine FAQ page using your top 3 customer questions — with direct, specific answers. This is high-value and most trade businesses don't have it
- Write one case study or customer outcome piece. Specific project, specific outcome, specific location. This is social proof that AI engines can cite

**Days 11–20: Platform Consistency**

Get your brand presence coherent across your key platforms.

- Update LinkedIn company page with your updated core description
- Post the case study across LinkedIn, Facebook, and Google Business (different formats for each, same substance)
- Set up a consistent posting cadence: commit to a frequency you can sustain — once a week is more valuable than twice a week for one month and then nothing
- Request 3–5 recent reviews from actual customers. Specific reviews (that mention what you did and where) are far more valuable than generic "great service" reviews

**Days 21–30: System and Measurement**

Move from setup to ongoing.

- Set up your AEO baseline measurement: track your business appearing in AI answers for your top 3 category + location queries. Manually test these in ChatGPT and Perplexity. Document where you stand today
- Build a simple content calendar for the next 90 days: one piece of expertise content per month, one case study per month, consistent platform posting throughout
- Review and refine your posting based on what performed. The system improves when you close the loop between what you published and what actually worked

**What success looks like at day 30:**

- Entity consistency across all platforms: done
- Core expertise content with citation-ready structure: live
- At least one case study published across platforms: live
- Consistent posting cadence established: running
- AEO baseline measured: documented

This isn't a complete AEO strategy. It's the foundation. The compounding starts from here — and it compounds faster than most business owners expect, because the ANZ trade category is still relatively underpopulated in AI search results.

The businesses who do this work in the next 12 months will be the default AI-cited authorities in their categories by 2027.

**Key Takeaway:** AEO doesn't require a marketing team or a large budget. It requires structured content, cross-platform consistency, and a system for maintaining both. Thirty days builds the foundation. The following months build the moat.
