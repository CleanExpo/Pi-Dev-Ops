## Post 2: Behind the Scenes
**Hook: "Behind the scenes — what the CCW CRM actually does"**

---

People hear "CRM" and think contact database. What CCW has is closer to an automated sales rep that never clocks off.

Here's what the system does on a normal trading day:

**Morning:** New orders from the overnight batch sync into the CRM. Customer records update automatically — last order date, product category, order value, lifetime spend.

**Throughout the day:** If a customer who hasn't ordered in 90 days visits the site and browses carpet extractors, a re-engagement sequence triggers. Not immediately — timed to land in business hours the next morning.

**After a sale:** A post-purchase sequence kicks off at day 3 (usage tips), day 14 (consumables reminder relevant to what they bought), and day 45 (reorder prompt based on product lifespan).

**On a customer's trade anniversary:** An automated touchpoint goes out — no discount, just acknowledgement. Open rates on these are significantly above average.

None of this requires a marketing hire to run. It runs on rules built into the platform. The CCW team reviews performance dashboards, adjusts sequences when needed, and gets on with the actual business of selling equipment.

The CRM isn't replacing human relationships in trade. It's making sure no relationship slips through the cracks because someone was too busy to follow up.

That's what a properly built trade CRM looks like.

## Post 3: The AI Layer
**Hook: "The AI that manages their customer segments automatically"**

---

Manual segmentation breaks down at scale. When you have thousands of customers across multiple product categories, someone has to decide who goes in which bucket — and keep those buckets updated as behaviour changes.

CCW's platform uses rule-based AI to handle this automatically.

The logic works like this:

**Segment assignment is dynamic.** A customer who buys a carpet extractor gets tagged as "carpet extractor owner." When they buy chemicals three months later, they cross into a new segment. Their communications shift to reflect the full picture of what they use.

**RFM scoring runs in the background.** Recency, Frequency, Monetary value — each customer gets a rolling score. High-value, high-frequency buyers get different treatment than a customer who bought once eighteen months ago. The system knows the difference. You don't have to manually sort it.

**Predictive reorder windows.** Based on average product lifespan and purchase history, the system surfaces customers likely to need consumables (chemicals, hoses, pads) in the next 30 days. The sales team sees this in a dashboard view, not buried in a spreadsheet.

**Churn risk flags.** Customers whose order frequency drops below their historical pattern get flagged. That triggers a manual or automated outreach depending on customer value tier.

The result: every customer is in the right segment, all the time, without a data analyst running pivot tables every week.

For trade distributors with product catalogues this is the difference between a CRM that collects data and one that uses it.

## Post 5: The Dashboard
**Hook: "The Unite-Hub dashboard they check every morning"**

---

The best CRM in the world doesn't help if the team doesn't actually use it.

The CCW team opens Unite-Hub first thing every morning. Here's what they're looking at:

**Top of screen: today's dispatch queue.** Orders that need to go out today, flagged by urgency. The same-day dispatch promise CCW makes to trade buyers is visible at a glance — how many orders are on track, how many need attention.

**Left panel: segment health.** Active customers (ordered in last 90 days), warming customers (last order 91–180 days), cold customers (180+ days). The trend line matters — if the cold bucket is growing, something is wrong.

**Centre: reorder opportunities.** Customers the system has flagged as likely to need product in the next 30 days, ranked by customer value. The team can action these with one click — assign a follow-up task or trigger a targeted email.

**Right: sequence performance.** Open rates, click rates, and conversion on each automated email sequence. If the 14-day consumables email is underperforming, the team knows before it becomes a problem.

**Bottom: trade account activity.** Key accounts — large cleaning businesses, facilities management companies, resellers — and when they last engaged, last ordered, and what their 90-day value trend looks like.

The whole thing takes about 10 minutes to review. Then the CCW team goes and sells equipment.
