## Post 1 — Educational / Authority
**Theme:** IAQ in Australian commercial buildings — NCC 2022 + the economic cost of poor air quality  
**Target audience:** Building managers, facility managers, property developers

---

Most building managers don't know they have an air quality problem. They just know their tenants are sick more often than they should be.

Here's what the data says about Australian commercial buildings:

Poor indoor air quality costs Australian businesses an estimated $12 billion annually in lost productivity, sick days, and increased absenteeism. That figure is from before COVID changed how we think about what we breathe indoors.

NCC 2022 tightened the ventilation requirements for Class 5, 6, 7, and 8 buildings. The code now mandates minimum outdoor air rates that many existing buildings simply can't meet without mechanical intervention. The installations were made to 2019 standards. The standards moved.

This matters for two reasons:

**One:** If your building was commissioned before the NCC 2022 update, you may have a compliance gap you don't know about. Not a fine-you-tomorrow compliance gap — but a you'll-find-out-when-a-tenant-complains gap.

**Two:** The economic argument for good IAQ is stronger than most people realise. A 1% improvement in cognitive function translates to measurable productivity gains for office-based tenants. A properly ventilated building commands higher lease rates and lower tenant churn. This is infrastructure, not a nice-to-have.

I've spent 25 years in this industry watching buildings fail not because owners didn't care — but because no one told them the ventilation system installed in 2017 was already behind the curve.

If you manage a commercial building in Australia and you haven't had a current IAQ assessment done, start there. The answers might surprise you.

Happy to point you in the right direction if you're not sure where to start.

## Post 2 — Expertise / Credentialing
**Theme:** Hard-won lesson from 25 years in building science — the one thing clients always get wrong  
**Target audience:** Restoration contractors, IICRC-certified professionals, building inspectors

---

Twenty-five years in this industry, and the mistake I see most often isn't technical.

It's documentation.

I've seen contractors do the remediation work perfectly — correct drying protocol, right equipment, proper containment — and then lose the insurance dispute because the moisture readings weren't taken correctly, weren't recorded systematically, or weren't tied to a defensible drying log.

IICRC S500 is explicit about this. You need pre-drying moisture content readings. You need psychrometric data during drying. You need final readings that demonstrate you've reached the standard. And you need all of it tied to the equipment you used and the dates it ran.

What I see in practice: photos on a phone, a couple of readings in a WhatsApp message, and a final "it's dry" sign-off.

That's not documentation. That's a liability.

The moisture meter you use matters. The calibration of that instrument matters. Whether it's a pin-type, pinless, or combination unit — and whether you're using the right wood-species correction factor — matters. I've reviewed insurance disputes where the contractor had done everything right physically and lost because they couldn't prove it.

The work is only as good as the paper trail.

This is why I built Moisture Meter Experts around professional-grade equipment and training, not just product sales. An instrument is only useful if the operator knows what the number means and how to defend it.

If you're an IICRC-certified restorer and you're not confident about your moisture documentation practice — that's the place to start.

## Post 3 — Product / Technical
**Theme:** LGR vs Desiccant dehumidification — the decision framework for restoration contractors  
**Target audience:** Restoration contractors, water damage professionals, equipment hire decision-makers

---

The LGR vs desiccant question comes up constantly. Let me give you the framework I use.

**Start with the psychrometrics, not the equipment.**

Low-grain refrigerant (LGR) dehumidifiers are workhorses. They perform best when ambient temperatures are between 16°C and 35°C, and relative humidity is above 50%. In those conditions — which covers most Australian water damage jobs — LGR is efficient, cost-effective, and easy to operate.

But conditions change. And when they do, LGR falls apart.

The moment you're working in an unheated structure in winter (say, a Melbourne property in July at 8°C ambient), LGR efficiency drops significantly. The refrigerant cycle can't run efficiently at low temperatures. You're burning energy and not extracting moisture.

**That's the desiccant case.**

Desiccant dehumidifiers use a hygroscopic material — typically silica gel or lithium chloride — to adsorb moisture regardless of temperature. They work at sub-zero temperatures. They're the right tool for:

- Low-temperature environments (below 15°C ambient)
- Very low humidity targets (below 35% RH) — common in hardwood floor drying
- Crawl spaces and subfloor areas where LGR struggles
- Structural drying where you need to pull moisture from materials, not just air

The trade-off: desiccants require a regeneration heat source and have higher energy consumption per litre extracted in warm conditions. If it's a warm, humid Australian summer job — LGR wins on efficiency and cost.

**The decision framework:**
1. What's the ambient temperature? Below 15°C → desiccant first.
2. What's the target RH? Below 35% → desiccant only.
3. What's the substrate? Dense materials, hardwood floors → desiccant advantage.
4. What's the job duration? Long drying times on a fixed structure → run the numbers on energy cost.

Most well-equipped restoration businesses run both. The question is which one goes in first.
