### Scene 2 — The Physics (Why Both Exist)
**Duration:** 0:45–2:15  
**Ivi voiceover:**
"Both machines do the same job: remove moisture from air. They just do it differently.

An LGR — low-grain refrigerant — works like your fridge. It chills a coil below dew point, moisture condenses, drips into a tank. Simple, efficient, and it works well when conditions are in range.

A desiccant works completely differently. It passes air over a hygroscopic material — usually silica gel or a similar compound — which chemically adsorbs moisture. No refrigerant cycle. No condensate. The moisture is captured in the material itself, then regenerated out with heat.

The physics matter because they determine when each machine works. And the conditions on a real job site are not always the conditions shown in the brochure."

### Scene 3 — Temperature: The Primary Decision Factor
**Duration:** 2:15–3:30  
**Ivi voiceover:**
"Temperature is the first decision point. Always.

LGR dehumidifiers work efficiently between approximately 16 and 35 degrees Celsius ambient. Below 16°C, efficiency drops. Below 10°C, many units start to ice up. You're running a machine that can't do what you need it to.

In Melbourne in July. In a subfloor in Hobart. In a commercial cool room. In a garage in Canberra in winter. In any of those scenarios, if you send in an LGR as your primary unit, you're fighting the physics.

Desiccants have no lower temperature limit on performance. They work at sub-zero. They're used in cold storage facilities, freezer rooms, even outdoor applications. If the job site is cold, desiccant isn't just better — it's the only tool that will actually achieve your drying targets."

### Scene 4 — Humidity Targets: The Second Decision Factor
**Duration:** 3:30–4:45  
**Ivi voiceover:**
"Second factor: what humidity level do you need to reach?

For most Category 1 water damage jobs — clean water, standard materials — you're targeting somewhere between 45 and 55% relative humidity. LGR handles that comfortably.

But hardwood floors are different. IICRC S500 and the Australian hardwood standards require you to get materials back to equilibrium moisture content. For most Australian hardwoods, that means driving the RH in the cavity below 35%, sometimes below 30%.

At 35% relative humidity, an LGR is working against its own limitations. The refrigerant cycle becomes less efficient at low vapour pressure. The machine runs but extracts very little.

Desiccants pull moisture effectively at any humidity level. They don't care if it's 80% or 15%. If your target is sub-35% RH, a desiccant is the only machine that will get you there efficiently."

### Scene 6 — Common Mistakes
**Duration:** 6:30–8:00  
**Ivi voiceover:**
"Let me cover three mistakes I see regularly.

One: using LGR in cold conditions and assuming it's working because the dial says it's running. The machine is running. It's just not extracting. Check your outlet grain depression. If it's minimal, you've got the wrong equipment.

Two: not accounting for regeneration heat in desiccant sizing. Desiccants require heat to regenerate the rotor. In enclosed spaces, that heat load increases the temperature. It's not a problem — but you need to plan your airflow so you're not running exhaust air into the drying zone.

Three: leaving LGR as the only unit when hardwood floors are involved. I've reviewed jobs where the contractor ran LGR for ten days, hit a wall at 42% RH, couldn't get lower, and called it dry. It wasn't. The hardwood wasn't back to EMC. The floor moved after the fact. Desiccant would have solved that in two additional days.

Know your tools. Know their limits."

### Scene 3 — What IICRC S500 Actually Requires
**Duration:** 2:30–4:30  
**Ivi voiceover:**
"IICRC S500 is the standard for professional water damage restoration. It's what Australian insurers reference. Here's what it actually requires for documentation.

Pre-drying readings: Before any equipment goes on site, you need moisture content readings on all affected materials. Pin-type readings for wood and organic materials. Relative humidity readings for ambient conditions. Surface and subsurface readings for concrete and masonry.

These establish your baseline. They're what you're comparing against when you demonstrate a completed drying outcome. Without a baseline, you have no endpoint.

During drying: Daily psychrometric readings — temperature, relative humidity, specific humidity (grains per pound or grams per kilogram). These tell you whether drying is progressing at the expected rate. If it's not, there's a reason — and that reason needs to be documented too.

Equipment records: Every piece of equipment on site, with model number, serial number, placement location, and operational hours logged daily. If the equipment list doesn't match the drying claim, it's a flag.

Final readings: Post-drying moisture content readings that demonstrate you've reached the target — typically EMC for the material type in the local climate. In Australia, equilibrium moisture content targets vary by state and season. This has to be in the log.

Sign-off: The log should be dated, signed, and where possible, witnessed or provided to the property owner at completion."

### Scene 4 — Choosing the Right Moisture Meter (and Why It Matters for Claims)
**Duration:** 4:30–6:30  
**Ivi voiceover:**
"The instrument you use is part of the documentation. Not just the reading — the instrument.

A calibration certificate matters. A consumer-grade moisture meter from a hardware store does not have a calibration certificate. An adjuster who knows what they're looking at will question readings taken with unverified instruments.

For restoration work in Australia, you need:
- A professional-grade pin-type meter for wood — one that has selectable species correction and a calibration verification method
- A pinless or radio-frequency meter for concrete, masonry, and surface scanning
- A thermo-hygrometer with data logging for ambient psychrometrics

The species correction factor is specific to Australia. Most pin-type meters default to Douglas Fir calibration — which is an American standard. Australian timber species have different density and moisture relationships. If you're not correcting for species, your readings can be off by several percentage points. That's the difference between a timber that's dry and one that isn't.

For high-value claims or complex losses, a multi-function meter — one that reads pin, pinless, and psychrometrics from the same unit — simplifies your data log significantly. One instrument, one calibration certificate, one chain of custody."

### Scene 6 — Digital Documentation vs Paper
**Duration:** 8:30–9:45  
**Ivi voiceover:**
"Paper works. It's defensible if it's systematic and consistent. But digital documentation has one significant advantage: timestamps.

Software like Encircle, Dash, or MICA creates automatically timestamped entries. The system logs when the reading was taken — not when you entered it. For dispute purposes, that's a meaningful chain of custody improvement.

The caveat: digital systems need to capture the same data fields as paper. If you're using a moisture management app that doesn't record instrument model or species correction, you've gained timestamping and lost calibration accountability. That's not an improvement.

Whatever system you use — paper or digital — the question is: if a loss adjuster sat down with this log twelve months from now, could they reconstruct exactly what was done, in what order, with what instruments, and verify that the drying outcome matches the documented readings? If yes — you're covered. If not — you're at risk."

### Scene 4 — The Six IAQ Failure Points
**Duration:** 4:00–6:00  
**Ivi voiceover:**
"In 25 years of site visits, I see the same failure points come up repeatedly. Here are the six most common.

One: Outdoor air dampers that have closed or drifted. Damper actuators fail. Linkages seize. The system runs, the fans operate, but the outdoor air rate drops to near zero. This is the single most common cause of elevated CO2 in commercial buildings.

Two: Return air recirculation without filtration. In economiser mode, many systems recirculate return air with minimal filtration. Fine for energy efficiency, not fine if occupant-generated contaminants — VOCs, bioaerosols — are being redistributed.

Three: Dirty coils and filters. A coil fouled with biological growth is not just a filtration failure — it's a source. Every air cycle picks up from it.

Four: Pressurisation imbalance. Buildings that are slightly negative pressure draw in uncontrolled infiltration — from carparks, loading docks, adjacent tenancies. You're ventilating, but you're not controlling what comes in.

Five: CO2 demand control ventilation that's over-weighted. DCV saves energy by reducing outdoor air when CO2 drops. But if the CO2 sensors are poorly placed or poorly calibrated, the system under-ventilates. Energy efficiency at the cost of air quality.

Six: Missing commissioning data. Many building managers don't have current commissioning reports. They have a certificate from 2017 and a verbal assurance that it's fine. That's not evidence."

### Scene 6 — What Good IAQ Management Looks Like
**Duration:** 7:30–9:00  
**Ivi voiceover:**
"A well-managed building's IAQ program has four components.

One: Annual commissioning verification. Confirm outdoor air rates are being delivered as designed, filters are replaced on schedule, and dampers and controls are operational. This generates a document that demonstrates due diligence.

Two: Continuous or regular spot monitoring. CO2 is the most practical proxy for ventilation sufficiency. A building with CO2 under 800ppm consistently is well ventilated for its occupancy. One reading above 1200ppm mid-afternoon in the main tenancy is a flag.

Four: Occupant feedback mechanism. A structured way for tenants to report IAQ concerns — separate from general maintenance requests — that routes to someone who can investigate. Not 'put in a help desk ticket' — an actual IAQ response protocol.

This isn't expensive at scale. The cost of good IAQ management is a fraction of the cost of a dispute, a sick building claim, or a lease break on grounds of habitability."

"One more thing worth noting: IAQ and sustainability are not in tension.

There's a common assumption that improving outdoor air rates costs energy. It does, if you're just opening dampers without considering heat recovery.

Energy recovery ventilation — ERV or HRV systems — allow you to increase fresh air delivery while recovering most of the thermal energy from exhaust air. For new commercial builds and major HVAC upgrades, this is now the standard approach.

NABERS Energy ratings, which most institutional tenants now require, don't penalise ventilation. They penalise waste. A well-designed ventilation system with heat recovery can meet or exceed current NCC 2022 outdoor air requirements without an energy penalty.
